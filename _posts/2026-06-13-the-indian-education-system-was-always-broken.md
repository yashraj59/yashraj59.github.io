The Indian education system was always broken.

It was broken ten years ago. It was broken twenty years ago. It is broken today. Nothing about it was ever built for everyone.

It was built for people who already had money and lived in cities and went to good schools and had someone at home who understood how the whole fucking system worked.

The rest of us were supposed to be grateful for whatever scraps we got and stay quiet.

And most of us did.

Now students are protesting over the NEET paper leak. They marched toward Parliament and the police answered them with barricades and lathis and tear gas.

Good.

I am glad students are angry and I am glad they are protesting. They should be furious. When one exam decides the future of millions of students and that exam is leaked, people should be on the streets.

But let us be honest about why this suddenly became a national crisis.

For decades nobody gave a shit about students from villages and small towns.

There were no national protests when a kid dropped out because his school had no teacher. There were no TV debates about schools that existed only on paper. There were no influencers crying into their phones when students spent years in classrooms with no lab and no library and no electricity and no internet and no teacher who actually showed up.

The system was unfair the whole time.

Everyone knew it.

But as long as it was only poor kids and rural kids and small-town students suffering, it was treated as normal. It was just how things were. Sad maybe, but not urgent.

Now the failure has reached students the media and the political parties and the urban middle class cannot ignore.

Suddenly it is a national emergency.

Suddenly everyone cares.

Where the fuck were you before?

## We Started the Race Years Behind

I am from a 3-4 tier town in Bihar.

Growing up we had almost no guidance. Nobody explained what NEET was, which in my time was called AIPMT, or what IIT was or which colleges existed or how entrance exams worked or what we were supposed to do after school.

We did not have career counsellors.

A lot of us did not have parents who had gone to college. There was nobody at home who could explain application forms or coaching or entrance tests or scholarships or admissions or career options.

By the time some of us even heard about these exams, city students had already been preparing for years.

Our schools had nothing. Half the time they did not have enough teachers. The teachers we did have were often teaching subjects they had never been trained to teach. Some did not show up for days. Sometimes weeks.

But on paper we were educated.

On paper the school existed.

On paper the teacher was employed.

On paper everything looked fine.

That is how this country works. The paperwork is perfect and the kid is fucked.

I got lucky.

People in my extended family had studied outside my town or state, and when they visited my town they guided me a little. They told me what was possible. They explained a few things. That small bit of guidance changed the direction of my life.

That is the only reason I am sitting here writing this.

And I know people who were probably ten times smarter than me who never got even that little bit of help.

They were sharp. They worked hard. They learned fast. They could have done well anywhere.

But nobody showed them the door.

Their families could not afford to move them to a city. They could not pay for coaching. They could not let them spend years preparing without earning money. They did not have someone at home who understood the system.

So they ended up with nothing.

Not because they had no talent.

Not because they were lazy.

Not because they did not deserve better.

The system just never cared enough to find out what they could do.

We love telling successful students that they earned everything through hard work. Hard work matters, but hard work is not magic.

Hard work cannot give you information you were never given.

Hard work cannot replace teachers you never had.

Hard work cannot pay coaching fees your family cannot afford.

Hard work cannot make electricity or internet or books or confidence or English or professional networks appear out of nowhere.

Being smart is not enough in this country when nobody shows you where the door is and your family cannot afford to open it.

## These Exams Do Not Measure Talent Alone

We talk about NEET and JEE like every student walks into the exam hall with an equal chance.

That is bullshit.

One student spends two or three years in a big coaching institute. They have specialised teachers and test series and doubt classes and performance analysis and updated study material and mock exams and a full preparation schedule.

Another student has one textbook and a schoolteacher who barely finished the syllabus and an internet connection that works when it feels like it.

Then they sit for the same exam and we call the result merit.

What a fucking joke.

The paper may be the same.

The preparation is not.

One kid was trained for the race from the start. The other kid found out there was a race when everyone else had already started running.

And then we praise the winner for being naturally gifted.

Do you know how hard it is to crack NEET with no coaching and no reliable teachers and no test prep and no guidance and no money?

These exams are not testing intelligence alone. They are testing how many years of preparation your family could afford. They are testing whether you lived near the right coaching centre. They are testing whether your parents could pay rent in another city. They are testing whether you could afford to fail once and try again.

Coaching costs lakhs.

Coaching mostly exists in cities.

So a student from a small town has to leave home and pay fees and pay rent and pay for food and gamble the savings of a whole family just to get the preparation richer students treat as normal.

Most families cannot even think about doing that.

So the same privileged group keeps winning the race, not because they are the only talented ones, but because they are the only ones who were properly allowed to train for it.

This does not mean every city student is rich. Of course not. Cities have poor students and first-generation students and migrants and families under huge pressure too.

But let us not pretend that geography and family income and school quality and language and parental education and social networks do not matter.

A clean exam cannot fix an unequal childhood.

## Stop Flaunting IIT and Harvard Like They Prove the System Works

Then I saw a CJP spokesperson on TV saying, "We are educated. We went to IIT and then Harvard or LSE."

No shit, Sherlock.

And how the fuck do you think you got into IIT in the first place?

You did not get there through talent alone.

You got there because you had access. You had information. You had coaching. You had support. You had schools that worked. You had people around you who knew the rules.

Maybe you also worked hard. Good for you. Nobody is denying that.

But hard work and privilege can exist at the same time.

Your degree is not the flex you think it is.

It is proof of exactly what I am talking about.

You are standing there as living proof that the system opens doors more easily for some people than others, and you think saying "IIT" and "Harvard" makes your argument untouchable.

It does not.

An elite degree may prove you achieved something. It does not prove the competition was fair.

Did you know about IIT from childhood?

Did your school prepare students for it?

Did your parents understand the process?

Could your family afford coaching?

Did you have a quiet room to study?

Did you have reliable electricity and internet?

Could you afford to try again if you failed once?

Those things matter.

So do not sit on TV and wave IIT and Harvard and LSE at me as proof that you deserve to be heard more than everyone else.

Fix the system that opened the door for you and slammed it shut on millions of others.

## Do Not Act Like You Just Found Out

So yes, you are protesting today.

Good. I mean it. This fight needs every voice it can get.

But do not act like you discovered educational injustice this week.

This did not start with a leaked paper. It was here the whole time. We watched brilliant students disappear because nobody helped them at the right time. We watched kids leave school to work. We watched parents borrow money for coaching that led nowhere. We watched students blame themselves for failures that were built long before they ever walked into an exam hall.

You were born in Delhi or Mumbai or Patna or some other big city, you went to a good school, you had coaching and internet and guidance and every resource in the world, and you never had to see any of this because the system was never coming for you. Now it came for you and suddenly it is real.

That is fine. But do not stop at your own rank. Do not make this only about the exam that cheated you. Use everything you have, your school and your English and your network and your platform, and point it at the kids who were cheated before they even knew there was a system.

That is what would make you different from every other person shouting outside Parliament.

## Stop Turning Students Into Political Props

And do not turn this into your party politics either, because that is exactly what everyone is doing.

Every party has shown up with its flags and slogans and leaders and microphones and cameras.

The students have become props.

Politicians who never gave a shit about rural schools are suddenly crying for students on TV. Leaders who ignored teacher shortages and broken classrooms and coaching nobody could reach now want to stand at the front of the march.

Everyone wants to use this moment for their own side.

Nobody wants to fix the thing underneath it.

Because the thing underneath it is hard. It is expensive. It takes years. It does not make one dramatic headline. It does not win an election next week.

Real reform means hiring teachers and training them and paying them and making sure they actually show up.

Real reform means building schools that work in places cameras never visit.

Real reform means giving a poor kid the same information and preparation and choices that a rich kid gets automatically.

That is much harder than shouting slogans outside Parliament.

## This Is Not Your Chance to Break My Country

And there is one more group I need to talk to.

The people who look at this protest and see a chance to start something else. The people who want a Nepal or a Bangladesh type situation here. The people who do not care about a single student and only want the country to burn so they can call it a revolution.

Fuck you.

I am with the students of this country. I am with the kid who studied for two years and got cheated by a leaked paper. I am with the parents who borrowed money and got nothing back. I am with every student who has a real reason to be angry.

I am not with you.

You are not fighting for students. You are hiding behind them. You saw real anger and you want to ride it toward something the students never asked for. You want to take a fight about a broken exam and turn it into a fight to break the country.

That is not the same thing and you know it.

A student wants a fair exam. You want chaos.

A student wants the system fixed. You want the system gone.

Do not pretend those are the same.

I will criticise this government all day. I will call out the Education Minister. I will say the system is broken because it is broken. That is not weakness. That is how you fix a country you actually care about.

But wanting it fixed and wanting it destroyed are two completely different things.

So take your revolution fantasy and your Nepal and Bangladesh comparisons and your fake concern for students somewhere else.

YOU WILL NEVER BE ABLE TO BREAK MY COUNTRY.

## And the Influencers Can Fuck Off Too

Then there are the influencers.

The ones uploading crying videos on Instagram with sad Swades or Rang De Basanti background music and captions about how their hearts are breaking for the students.

You do not care.

You saw a trending topic.

You saw suffering you could turn into engagement.

You saw a chance to make yourself look kind.

You did not say a word when nobody was watching, and you will go silent the second the trend is over.

Today you will post a reel about students.

Tomorrow you will be back to selling protein powder or skincare or gambling apps or whatever other bullshit is paying you that week.

That is not awareness.

That is performance.

If your compassion lasts exactly as long as the news cycle, it was never compassion.

So take your fake crying face and your sad music and your carefully edited outrage and fuck off.

## We Have Seen This Before

This is not the first paper leak.

It will not be the last, unless something real changes.

We have seen this exact drama play out already.

Everyone shouts for a few weeks. Committees are announced. Politicians blame each other. TV anchors scream. A few arrests are made. Maybe there is a re-exam.

Then the news moves on and everyone moves on with it.

The students get some administrative fix and go back to their lives. The villages stay where they were. The schools stay broken. The teachers stay absent. The coaching stays unaffordable. The poor stay invisible.

Until the next scandal.

And every time, we are told this is just how it is. Poor people suffering quietly and far away gets treated as normal, as background, as something you are supposed to accept and move on from.

I am not moving on from it.

That cycle only keeps running because we let it go quiet every single time. So this time do not let it go quiet. Do not let it turn into one more scandal that everyone forgets by next month. Keep the pressure on until something real changes underneath, because the only reason nothing ever changes is that we stop pushing before it does.

## Fixing NEET Alone Will Not Do Shit

If you actually want to fix something, fix the education system.

And I do not mean one committee and one investigation and one press conference and one minister giving a speech about transparency.

Fix the schools that have no teachers.

Put qualified teachers in them. Pay them properly. Train them. Make them show up.

Fix the schools with no lab and no library and no toilet and no electricity and no internet.

Give students career guidance before it is too late for them to use it.

Make good preparation available publicly and for free in many languages.

A poor kid should not have to leave home and spend money his family does not have just to get a fair shot at one exam.

And stop acting like medicine and engineering are the only respectable careers in this country.

Give kids real options.

Tell them about research and teaching and nursing and design and technology and public service and skilled trades and farming and starting a business and everything else that exists beyond doctor and engineer.

Education is supposed to open up a kid's options.

Instead we have reduced millions of lives to two exams.

Fixing NEET alone will not do shit.

You can make the paper perfectly secure tomorrow and the kid from my town still will not have a good teacher. He still will not have coaching. He still will not have guidance. He still will not know what options exist until it is too late.

The exam is not the disease.

The real problem is everything sitting underneath it.

The exam is just where years of inequality get turned into a rank and then handed to us as merit.

## Kids Are Carrying the Shame of Whole Families

And it is not only the schools and the money and the coaching.

There is another pressure nobody wants to talk about honestly, and that is the pressure from home and from society.

From the time a kid is young he is told he has to become a doctor or an engineer and that almost nothing else counts as success.

Every relative is watching.

Every neighbour is comparing marks.

Every family gathering turns into an interrogation.

"How much did he score?"

"Which coaching did she join?"

"Did he crack NEET?"

"Did she get IIT?"

Kids are treated like entries on a family scoreboard.

If you do not crack NEET or JEE you are not just treated as someone who failed one exam. You are treated as though you failed your family.

Your parents are questioned.

Your relatives gossip.

Your neighbours compare you to someone else's child.

So the student is not even studying for himself anymore.

He is studying so his parents do not have to feel ashamed.

He is studying because the family borrowed money.

He is studying because his father sold land.

He is studying because his mother gave up her savings.

He is studying because everyone told him this one exam is the only way out.

Half these kids are not carrying their own dreams.

They are carrying the dreams and the fear and the debt and the pride and the social status of a whole family.

And when they break under that weight, everyone acts shocked.

What the fuck did you think would happen?

A kid cannot carry the future of five people and the expectations of twenty relatives and the shame put on him by a whole neighbourhood without breaking somewhere.

This has to change too.

Not through another exam reform.

Not through another motivational speech.

Parents need to be told that their child is worth more than one rank.

Kids need to be allowed to choose their own path.

A poor kid is already carrying enough.

He should not also have to carry the fear that one result will destroy his family's dignity.

## BJP, This Happened on Your Watch

And BJP, this happened on your watch.

Paper leak after paper leak. Exam after exam. Failure after failure.

Get your shit together and own it.

A government cannot take credit for every achievement and then act like repeated institutional failures are nobody's responsibility.

Leadership means accountability.

It means someone at the top loses their job when a system that affects millions of students collapses.

Dharmendra Pradhan has failed these students.

He is still sitting in his chair.

Why?

An education minister does not need to personally leak a paper to be responsible for the failure of the education system under him.

That is what ministerial responsibility means.

To the Prime Minister, if you actually care about these students, sack your Education Minister.

Today.

That is the bare minimum.

It will not fix the whole system. It will not repair decades of inequality. But it will set one basic principle that seems to have disappeared from Indian politics.

When institutions fail millions of people, somebody in power has to pay a price.

After that, start the real work.

Build an education system where a kid's future is not decided by his PIN code or his father's salary or his English or his caste network or his access to coaching or whether someone in his family knows how admissions work.

The students protesting today deserve justice.

But so do the millions of students who disappeared quietly from the system before anyone learned their names.

And if you want to come after me for using AI to write this, then yes, I used AI to help me write it.

I grew up without the kind of schooling that teaches you to write clean English, and I used a tool to close that gap. That gap is the whole thing this piece is about.

But the ideas are mine and the anger is mine and the experience is mine. AI did not give me any of that. So if the best you can do is attack the tool I used and not a single thing I said, then you have already proved my point for me.
